...to my inspiration

Snowflakes are a beautiful creation from nature. Due to the dipole behaviour of water molecules and the arrangement of the oxygen and hydrogen atoms, snowflakes are made up of a grid structure that results in symmetrical growth along 6 axis arranged in a hexagon. As there are numerous parameters influencing the shape of the snowflake (temperature, humidity, pressure, ...) as they fall down from the sky, it is said that every snowflake is unique.

...to the mathematical model for the snowflake shape

The shape has been created using a symmetrical probabilistic L-system. This video shows the generation process.

L-system

The L-system is a system of rules that results in patterns when translated. The used L-system is the following for drawing one snowflake-branch. INSERT PICTURE FROM LATEX

Probability

The production rules P are chosen based on a normal distribution. This ensures the snowflakes are different, but generally similar.

Symmetry

The generated branch is 6 times, each time rotating 60 degrees more. This results in a rotational symmetry group of size 6, and often of an additional reflective symmetry group as well.

...to the mathematical model of snowfall motion

Snowflakes do not fall straight from the sky. Due to their light weight, they move also in a horizontal direction while falling down.

...to the artwork

The artwork is an interactive animation that generates a number of different snowflake patterns resulting from the probabilistic L-system and depicts these as falling snow. The user can interact with the application by clicking on snowflakes to inspect them more closely. However, the snowflake will disappear shortly after, as it melts from the body heat. By clicking the button, you can download the artwork. It contains an executable and the snowflake images.
